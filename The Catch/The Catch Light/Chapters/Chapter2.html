Chapter 2 – Survive The Lie

Otti stood at the edge of the theatre like she’d taken a wrong turn into someone else’s future.

The room was massive. Vaulted ceilings. Tiered rows of seats filled with strangers. No one looked directly at her, but she felt it anyway. Watched. Or maybe worse—scanned.

The man who had called her earlier stood at the bottom, gesturing politely. “Ah, Miss Ottiline. This way, please.”

His voice echoed just slightly too long, like the room didn’t know how to handle warmth.

The lights dimmed before she took a step. A soft chime rang out. Cheerful. Fake.

A screen descended behind the stage with mechanical grace. The space filled with an unsettlingly smooth voice, the kind made for automated customer service lines.

“Introducing SIVIL, a civic system for a new generation.”

The entire theatre shifted. Not physically, but emotionally. Every head tilted upward. No one whispered. No one fidgeted. The silence was curated.

Warm colors flickered across the screen—a family picnicking in a green park, a smiling student paying less for university tuition, a construction worker receiving a bonus. The kind of montage designed to feel comforting, but distant. Too polished to be real.

“With SIVIL, participation has never been more rewarding. Complete your civic tasks and earn up to fifty percent discounts on tax, government services, and even partner storefronts.”

A montage followed: people scanning wristbands to access the metro, a mother collecting free groceries, an elderly woman smiling as her hospital fees vanished.

“Smart living means smart benefits. And the more consistent your contributions, the more the system works for you.”

Then the colors faded. The screen darkened.

A glowing red triangle blinked gently in the centre.

“Of course, failure to meet your obligations may result in temporary penalties. Delays can increase service costs, limit travel, or flag your household for review.”

A man on screen tried scanning into a building. Access Denied. Then again, later, now compliant, access granted.

“But rest assured: these are not punishments. They are motivators. Reminders that in a balanced society, no effort should go unrewarded.”

The lights brightened. Not warmly—just enough to see again. The screen vanished into the ceiling with surgical efficiency.

Another tone sounded, clean, digital, like an elevator had arrived somewhere behind her.

“Leader Assignments will now be issued.”

A pause.

Then: “Guild 7 — Leader: Ottiline Quandt.”

Her name lit the screen in white against stark black. Somewhere behind her, someone shifted in their seat.

Otti didn’t move.

The man at the bottom smiled again. Polite. Professional. Cold. “Miss Quandt, your Guild is now forming. Please follow the assistant through the left corridor. Your team awaits.”

Team.

That word didn’t sit right.

Otti didn’t respond. Just walked. Her boots echoed on polished tile like they weren’t part of her.

The assistant waiting for her was young, unreadable, almost doll-like in her blankness. No badge. No name. Only a single blinking dot on the collar of her blazer.

She didn’t speak as she led Otti through a long hallway—pale grey, walls matte and soft-lit. Cameras perched in every corner, subtle and watching. The silence was oppressive. Everything was designed to feel unthreatening. Which, somehow, made it worse.

They stopped in front of a sealed metal door.

“This is your team room. Once introductions are complete, your ID card will be issued,” the assistant said.

Otti nodded.

The assistant didn’t smile. She just looked at Otti for a moment longer than necessary—as if trying to calculate whether she’d break or not—then turned and walked away.

The door hissed open.

The room inside was colder. No windows. Concrete. A low blue light buzzed from above, constant and sterile.

A long metal table stretched across the centre. Ten chairs. Nine of them already filled.

When Otti stepped in, the room fell instantly quiet.

“Guild 7: All members present. Leader recognized: Ottiline Quandt.”

The speaker overhead spat out her name like a warning.

She scanned the faces around the table. A boy with wire-frame glasses stared like she was a complex equation waiting to be proven wrong. A girl with pink streaks in her hair leaned back like she owned the air. A man with shoulders too broad for the chair didn’t even flinch. A kid looked like sixteen kept their eyes locked to the metal tabletop. A woman in posture too perfect to be anything but ex-military sat still, unreadable.

They weren’t teammates.

They weren’t strangers.

They were pieces of a test no one agreed to take.

“You’ve got to be kidding me,” Otti muttered under her breath.

No one laughed.

But the pink-haired girl smirked. “You don’t look like much of a leader either.”

From the end of the table, the man chuckled. “She’s the one who rerouted the evac last night, right? Cold move. I respect it.”

“Guild 7 was assembled for behavioural diversity,” the speaker announced. “Purpose: Observe cohesion under ideological friction. Projected failure rate: 82%.”

A wall screen blinked on. Ten files opened side by side. Photos. Stats. Personality reports. Everyone’s face. Including hers.

“You gave them access to my file?” Otti asked the ceiling.

“Transparency breeds trust.”

“Transparency breeds surveillance,” the boy with glasses muttered.

Otti stepped forward and stood at the head of the table. She didn’t sit.

“I don’t trust any of you. You don’t trust me. That’s fine.”

The military woman finally spoke. “Name’s Adler. I’ll follow orders that make sense. The ones that don’t, I’ll override.”

Otti didn’t blink. “Good. Saves me time deciding who to break first.”

That shut down the room.

No challenge. No applause.

But something shifted—not fear, not agreement, just recognition.

The cameras stayed on.

Some people dozed in corners, still half-alert. Others leaned on folded arms. Otti didn’t sit. She cross-legged herself by the sealed door, staring up at the camera above.

She wasn’t trying to sleep.

“You think if we kill each other in here, it counts as data too?” she asked aloud.

A crackle responded from the speaker. “All variables are logged.”

She smirked. “Then you picked the wrong variables.”

Above, someone tagged her profile:
[Hostile sarcasm – Unstable leadership trend.]
[Survival probability: increased.]

At dawn, the door unsealed with a mechanical sigh. No goodbyes. No parting notes. Just release.

Otti walked out alone. She didn’t look back.

She stepped outside into a world still waking up, slipped on her helmet, and kicked the engine of her bike alive. It snarled beneath her.

But she didn’t head home. Not yet.

She rode until the streets were nearly empty. Until cafés were still closed and windows still slept.

At the edge of the district, tucked between two closed flower shops, was a bookstore with a crooked awning and a dusty bell above the door.

Otti parked. She stepped inside, her boots clinking on hardwood.

Behind the counter, an older man looked up from a battered typewriter. “Back already?”

“Just passing through,” she said.

He nodded toward the side wall. “Same copy of The Individual & The Structure is still waiting. You’re the only one who touches it.”

“That’s because no one else reads footnotes like gospel.”

“Or maybe they’re just scared of context.”

She smiled faintly.

In the back, she pulled the book from the shelf, flipped to the hollowed-out centre, and retrieved a worn notebook.

Inside: codes. Names. Crossed-out locations. Underlines. At the very bottom, in Gant’s handwriting:
We don’t have to fight loudly. We just have to survive longer than the lie.

Otti stared at the line. Then pulled out her pen, and wrote beneath it:
And plant something that outlives it.

She returned the book to the shelf and left the store without a word.

By the time she reached her bike again, the city had begun to breathe. People trickled onto sidewalks. Bus engines coughed awake. The illusion of normal resumed.

Her phone buzzed just before she reached the villa gates. Eloy.

She answered. “Hey.”

“Hey yourself,” he said. Light, but clipped. “You good?”

“I’m alive.”

“Good enough. You vanished after the call. Thought they might’ve absorbed you.”

“They tried. I walked out.”

A pause. “You sound tired.”

“I am.”

“You wanna talk?”

After a while. She slowed the bike at the villa gates.

She parked, killed the engine, and walked toward the door. It didn’t creak, but it never needed to.

Inside, the house looked like a photo someone left in the sun too long. Faded. Frozen.

Her parents sat exactly as she knew they would—her mother with her delicate coffee grip, her father with a newspaper he never finished.

They didn’t look up. She didn’t say anything.

She moved through the room like a ghost.

Upstairs, she paused at Gant’s door, knocked once, and let herself in.

Her sister was under a thin blanket, eyes half-lidded.

“You’re home,” Gant murmured.

“Yeah.”

“I kept your side cold for you.”

Otti smiled.

She brought her phone back to her ear. “I’m here,” she said.

On the other end, Eloy exhaled. “Good. Just text me if anything gets weird.”

“Things are always weird.”

“Fine. Weirder.”

“Okay.”

She looked at Gant. “I’ll talk to you later.”

She ended the call. Slipped the phone into her pocket.

The room was still heavy with things neither of them had the strength to say.

“Gnat,” Otti said. “School. Move.”

A groan. “Let me finish sleeping...”

Otti stared at the blanket. “This girl—” she muttered.

“Fine. I’m up. Shut up.”

“Sure.”

Otti turned to her room. Pulled out a fitted grey turtleneck, her usual jacket. Functional. Sharp. Nothing fancy. Nothing soft.

She clipped the SIVIL band onto her wrist.
Status: Active – Leader Access Verified.

She stared. Then shut it off.

Helmet. Bag. Boots.

She opened the door and saw Gant. Behind her: “Don’t forget to eat, ghost girl.”

“Don’t forget to breathe, swamp slug.”

The door clicked shut.

Downstairs, everything looked the same.

Otti didn’t stop.

She stepped into the sunlight.

And the day began.
